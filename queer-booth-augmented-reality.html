Queer Booth Augmented Reality



Client // Queer Booth Ltd

Duration // 2 Weeks (Feb 2020)

Deliverable // Hi-Fi Prototype

Tools // Figma, Photoshop, Spark AR

Team // Amanda Low & Hanna Hong
Background

2015 //

The original Queer Booth app was launched in 2015 by Jo Webber. After becoming aware of the extent of homophobic bullying when working with a youth charity, Jo wanted to normalise queerness and make education fun. Photo booth style apps were popular at the time, and noticing a gap in the market for an LGBTQ+ version, she decided to develop Queer Booth, to promote inclusiveness for LGBTQ+ youth.

2020 //

After pausing development of the app in 2016 due to resources, Jo was inspired in 2020 to relaunch Queer Booth by the new wave of LGBTQ+ apps. Hanna and I chose to work on the Queer Booth redesign after Jo submitted it for the Ironhack UX/UI Bootcamp students' final project round.


Problem


Technology //

With Photo Booth apps now outdated, the app needed to be updated to fit current technology. Inspired by Bitmojis, Facebook Stickers, queer dating apps, and SnapChat selfie filters, Jo was excited to see how new technology could shape the app.
Stereotypes //

While the original app had showcased outfits on what could now be viewed as stereotypes of gay and lesbian identities, Jo wanted the new version to be inclusive for all on the LGBTQ+ spectrum. Jo was open to a complete design overhaul - her only wish was to keep the hot pink of the logo.
LGBTQ+ Inclusivity //

Despite an increase of visibility, the original problem of bullying and lack of inclusion of LGBTQ+ people still existed in 2020. To gain insight on how the new version would address this problem, we decided to focus on the research.


Research




Coming out //

According to 2019 research study by Tinder: 75% of LGBTQ+ Generation Z individuals decide to come out online before coming out in real life (compared to just 1 in 5 of all LGBTQ+ individuals surveyed). Indicating the important role social media plays in providing a safe place for LGBTQ+ youth.

Online Behaviour //

We collected 140 survey responses and interviewed eight individuals from the LGBTQ+ community on how they expressed themselves online.

Market Positioning




Inner and Outer Expression //

We aimed to create the new Queer Booth app as a place to share stories without judgement, with LGBTQ+ themed selfie filters, keeping with the Photo Booth element. So users could express their inner and outer selves.

TikTok //

While the old Queer Booth was positioned closely to Snapchat, with a focus more on playful content. However, the new Queer Booth would be positioned more closely to TikTok. As a Social Entertainment network, TikTok fuses user-generated entertainment with cultural issues. Additionally, young LGBTQ+ users feel safer on TikTok, as their parents are not using it.
Features


Feature 1: AR Selfie Filters


From our interviews, top requests for filters included: Pride and celebrity themed filters. Asked to name their LGBTQ+ celebrity icons, many interviewees suggested pop star Lady Gaga.

To prototype the selfie filters, we looked into Spark AR Studio - a Facebook developed software used for creating AR effects. Hanna and I then spent a weekend teaching ourselves how to use Spark AR, resulting in five fun effects.
Feature 2: Sharing Stories


For many LGBTQ+ people, starting to explore and express their identities is done carefully, either because they are unsure, or their environment makes it difficult.

We imagined ways that users could share experiences, and find solidarity, in a safe environment. We thought that a feature allowing users to share stories anonymously would assist this, similar to PostSecret, Whisper and Jodel,
Ideation
Concept: Inner and Outer Expression


While most users tested knew how the two features worked, we needed to make it clearer why we combined them together.

After testing, it was clear that the concept of inner and outer self-expression had to be explained during the launch, resulting in the three card introduction explaining the concept of Queer Booth.
Naming: Memorable vs Understandable


We wanted to find names for our two main features that would be memorable, and easy to understand. Initially, we quite liked:
  • Photo Booth
  • Confession Booth
However, after testing, users pointed out that, for some, going to church confession was done more out of religious guilt, than for self expression.

After further brainstorming and testing, we decided to replace Confession Booth with Story Booth.
Ideation
Security: Fight Against Bullying


One negative aspect of anonymous social media is that it can be a breeding ground for bullying. Requiring parental consent to sign-up would restrict users self-expression, so we instead implemented a self -regulating minimum age.

There is an anonymous user ID, and no email address or phone number is required. All user data is stored in the app.

However, we decided against a private messaging feature (users may only publicly post comments). Keeping comments public would also help to regulate bullying.
Navigation: Swiping vs Menu


To navigate the Story Booth news feed, we took inspiration from Facebook and Instagram. The feed is the start page, where users can either scroll through posts or search using keywords.

To access the Photo Booth camera, users have to swipe left. Users can then scroll through the selfie filter options by swiping the camera button left, and then can save photos to their camera roll.

To go back to the Story Booth, users have to swipe right, and then to access the User Account users swipe right again.
UI
Story Feed Navigation


As a backup for those who might not find the swiping navigation intuitive, we added a menu in the Story Booth Feed to navigate to the Photo Booth and User Account.

We used three icons from the Detective-Glyph collection for the menu (these are also used in the introduction):
  • Photo Booth
  • Story Booth
  • User Account
Typography & Colour


For the logo and header fonts we chose Gilbert, named after Gilbert Baker, the creator of the rainbow flag.

We kept hot pink as a prominent colour in the font and as a highlight colour. We decided on a black background in order to make the rainbow colours of the logo pop.

The users' posts design was kept simple to facilitate quick posting - users may only choose the colour of the text.
Prototype
Real-Life Joy


Once we had the hi-fi prototype screens done we then had to prototype our Spark AR selfie filters. Hanna was our model for the prototype and her joyful reaction in the video is real - "Shane" is her favourite character from the show "The L-Word".

As a last-minute addition, we added a Queer Booth watermark to selfies so that when shared on external social media it would bring more users to Queer Booth.
Real-Life Inspiration


For the posts displayed in the Story Booth feed, we took inspiration from our user interviews and from other anonymous social media.

We wanted to demonstrate the safe environment created by Queer Booth v2, which allows users to express themselves freely, and to find representation.
Learnings
Creativity vs Guidelines


Jo was open to all of our ideas which meant a lot of creative freedom. However having fewer guidelines meant that we often had to find a balance between brainstorming wildly, and finding a logical solution to the problem.

Our user research was therefore very important in our decision-making process, and we kept coming back to the data to give us direction whenever our brainstorming sessions got out of hand.
Reflection


The main takeaway from this project is the importance of thorough research. As LGBTQ+ self-expression exists on a wide spectrum, creating something meaningful as well as inclusive, required conducting research and data sorting with sensitivity. A solid research base was our guiding light in times of tough decisions and made the solution we developed all the more meaningful.
