Queer Booth: Augmented Reality
Queer Booth Ltd



Tools: Figma, Photoshop, Spark AR


Deliverable: Hi-Fi Prototype


Time Span: Feb 2020 (2 Weeks)

Team: Amanda Low & Hanna Hong


Background: Queer Booth v1

The original Queer Booth app was launched in 2015 by Jo Webber. At the time, photo booth style apps were popular and she noticed a gap in the market for a version that catered to the LGBTQ+ community.

Also, aware of the extent of homophobic bullying after working with a youth charity, Jo wanted to normalise queerness and make education fun. In 2016 she paused further development of the app due to lack of resources.

Problem

Problem: Outdated Tech

Inspired by the new wave of LGBTQ+ apps, Jo wanted to relaunch Queer Booth - her passion project. However the app needed to be updated to fit current technology - with augmented reality (AR) selfie filters now all the rage.

While the original app had showcased outfits on what could now be viewed as stereotypes of gay and lesbian identities, Jo wanted the new version to be inclusive for all on the LGBTQ+ spectrum.
Task: Redesign for Relaunch

Hanna and I chose to work on the Queer Booth redesign after Jo submitted it for the Ironhack UX/UI Bootcamp students' final project round.

Inspired by Bitmojis, Facebook Stickers, queer dating apps, and SnapChat selfie filters, she was excited to see how the new technology could be utilised.

Jo was open to a complete design overhall - however she wanted the hot pink of the original logo to remain. It was also important to her that the user experience was seamless, and the target audience remain Generation Z.

Research
Survey: Self Expression


Our research aim was to collect data from the LGBTQ+ community regarding self expression of sexual orientation and gender identity.

In over 3 days we received over 140 responses, and the main trends were:
  • 65% of Generation Z participants thought that selfies are a good form of LGBTQ+ self expression.
  • 68% of participants share selfies on Messaging Apps
  • 50% express their sexual orientation by sharing content online
Interviews: Representation


The eight interviewiees' attitudes varied depending on their background and stage of "coming out". Nonethless we wanted our solution to be inclusive of all the opinions heard.

After an intense card sorting session, Hanna and I narrowed down two major areas of concern:
  1. Self Expression
  2. Representation
Social media plays a large role in LGBTQ+ representation, and the people that needed the most visiblity were the ones just starting on their "journey".
Users & Market
User Personas: Generation Z vs Millenials


For the primary user persona we created "Nick", who represents a Generation Z user who searches for LGBTQ+ connection online.

For the secondadry persona we created "Vivien", who represents a Millenial user who uses her online presence to promote LGBTQ+ issues.
Market Positioning: TikTok Comparison


A market analysis of popular apps with AR selfie filters revealed that that the closest competitor for the Queer Booth relaunch would be TikTok. LGBTQ+ users also feel safer using TikTok, as their parents aren’t using it yet.

Our aim was to create the new Queer Booth app as a place to share stories without judgement, with LGBTQ+ themed selfie filters. So users can express their inner and outer selves.
Features


Feature 1: AR Selfie Filters


From our interviews, top requests for filters included: Pride and celebrity themed filters. Asked to name their LGBTQ+ celebrity icons, many interviewees suggested popstar Lady Gaga.

To prototype the selfie filters, we looked into Spark AR Studio - a Facebook developed software used for creating AR effects. Hanna and I then spent a weekend teaching ourselves how to use Spark AR, resulting in five fun effects.
Feature 2: Sharing Stories


For many LGBTQ+ people, starting to explore and express their identities is done carefully, either because they are unsure or their environment makes it difficult.

We imagined ways that users could share experiences, and find solidarity, in a safe environment. We thought that a feature allowing users to share stories anonymously would assist this, similar to PostSecret, Whisper and Jodel,
Ideation
Concept: Inner and Outer Expression


While most users tested knew how the two features worked, we needed to make it clearer why we combined them together.

After testing, it was clear that the concept of inner and outer self expression had to be explained during the launch, resulting in the three card introduction explaining the concept of Queer Booth.
Naming: Memorable vs Understandable


We wanted to find names for our two main features that would be memorable, and easy to understand. Initially we quite liked:
  • Photo Booth
  • Confession Booth
However after testing, users pointed out that, for some, going to church confession was done more out of religious guilt, than for self expression.

After further brainstorming and testing we decided to replace Confession Booth with Story Booth.
Ideation
Security: Fight Against Bullying


One negative aspect of anonymous social media is that it can be a breeding ground for bullying. Requiring parental consent in order to sign-up would restrict users self expression, so we instead implemented a self regulating minimum age.

There is an anonymous user ID, and no email address or phone number is required. All user data is stored in the app.

However, we decided against a private messaging feature (users may only publicy post comments). Keeping comments public would also help to regulate bullying.
Navigation: Swiping vs Menu


To navigate the Story Booth news feed, we took inspiration from Facebook and Instagram. The feed is the start page, where users can either scroll through posts or search using keywords.

To access the Photo Booth camera, users have to swipe left. Users can then scroll through the selfie filter options by swiping the camera button left, and then can save photos to their camera roll.

To go back to the Story Booth, users have to swipe right, and then to access the User Account users swipe right again.
UI
Story Feed Navigation


As a backup for those who might not find the swiping navigation intuitive, we added a menu in the Story Booth Feed to navigate to the Photo Booth and User Account.

We used three icons from the Detective-Glyph collection for the menu (these are also used in the introduction):
  • Photo Booth
  • Story Booth
  • User Account
Typography & Colour


For the logo and header fonts we chose Gilbert, named after Gilbert Baker, the creator of the rainbow flag.

We kept hot pink as a prominent colour in the font and as a highlight colour. We decided on a black background in order to make the rainbow colours of the logo pop.

The users' posts design was kept simple to faciliate quick posting - users may only choose the colour of the text.
Prototype
Real-Life Joy


Once we had the hi-fi prototype screens done we then had to prototype our Spark AR selfie filters. Hanna was our model for the prototype and her joyful reaction in the video is real - "Shane" is herfavourite character from the show "The L-Word".

As a last minute addition we added a Queer Booth watermark to selfies so that when shared on external social media it would bring more users to Queer Booth.
Real-Life Inspiration


For the posts diplayed in the Story Booth feed, we took inspiration from our user interviews and from other anonymous social media.

We wanted to demonstrate the safe environment created by Queer Booth v2, which allows users to express themselves freely, and to find representation.
Learnings
Creativity vs Guidelines


Jo was open to all of our ideas which meant a lot of creative freedom. However having fewer guidelines meant that we often had to find a balance between brainstorming wildly, and finding a logical solution to the problem.

Our user research was therefore very important in our decision making process, and we kept coming back to the data to give us direction whenever our brainstorming sessions got out of hand.
Reflection


The main takeaway from this project is the important of thorough research. As LGBTQ+ self expression exists on a wide spectrum, creating something meaningful as well as inclusive, required conducting research and data sorting with sensitivity. A solid research base was our guiding light in times of tough decisions, and made the solution we developed all the more meaningful.
