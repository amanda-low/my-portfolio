Financial Planning App

Designing an intuitive mobile experience that helps young professionals build healthy financial habits without the overwhelm.

Role UX Designer
Timeline 4 months
Team 2 designers, 3 engineers
Methods User Interviews, Prototyping, Usability Testing

Overview

The Challenge

A fintech startup wanted to help millennials and Gen Z users take control of their finances. Existing budgeting apps were either too complex (spreadsheet-like) or too simplistic (lacking actionable insights). We needed to find the sweet spot.

My Contribution

I led the UX design for the mobile app, from initial research through to final UI specifications. This included defining the information architecture, creating interaction patterns, and conducting usability testing with target users.

Research & Discovery

We interviewed 18 potential users aged 22-35 about their relationship with money and existing financial tools. Key findings:

  • Emotional barriers: Many users felt shame or anxiety about checking their finances, leading to avoidance
  • Information overload: Detailed categorization felt like homework rather than helpful guidance
  • Lack of context: Users wanted to know "am I doing okay?" not just "what did I spend?"

Key personas: The Anxious Avoider and The Aspiring Saver

Process

Design Principles

Based on research, I established three design principles to guide our decisions:

  1. Reduce anxiety: Use encouraging language and progress-focused metrics
  2. Simplify ruthlessly: Show only what matters most, when it matters
  3. Build habits: Design for regular, brief check-ins rather than deep analysis sessions

Iterative Prototyping

I created three rounds of prototypes, testing each with 5-6 users. Early versions were too game-like (felt unserious), while others were too clinical. The final direction found a balance—calm and supportive without being childish.

Dashboard evolution across three design iterations

Solution

Smart Dashboard

The home screen surfaces one key insight per day rather than overwhelming data. A simple "pulse" indicator shows financial health at a glance—green, yellow, or red—with context about what's driving that status.

Gentle Nudges

Instead of rigid budgets, the app uses pattern recognition to suggest small behavior changes. "You usually spend less on weekdays—want to save the difference?"

Milestone Celebrations

The app recognizes progress—first month under budget, emergency fund started, debt milestone reached—reinforcing positive behaviors.

Final designs: Dashboard, daily insight, and milestone celebration

Outcome

The app launched in beta with 2,000 users. After 3 months:

  • 4.2x higher daily active usage compared to competitor benchmarks
  • 67% of users reported feeling less anxious about their finances
  • App Store rating of 4.7 stars with praise for simplicity
  • $18/month average savings increase among active users

Reflection

This project taught me the power of emotional design. The technical functionality of showing spending data is the same as competitors—the differentiation came from understanding and designing for how users feel.

One thing I'd explore further: personalization. Our one-size-fits-all approach worked well for most users, but we saw some who wanted more detail and control. A future iteration could adapt to different financial personality types.
