# Mobile Search

**Role:** Product Designer (Vision, UX & Design)
**Timeline:** Q1 2026
**Scope:** iOS, Android | HelloFresh
**Ownership:** Discovery, Definition, Design, Development

---

## The Challenge

HelloFresh's mobile app lacked search functionality. Users who knew what they wanted—a specific recipe, ingredient, or product—had no way to find it directly. They were forced to browse through categories and scroll through endless product cards.

Search had already been validated on web in the US and Canada, showing strong engagement. The goal for mobile was clear: bring search to the app with a focus on improving result relevance.

## The Opportunity

This wasn't just about adding a search bar. It was an opportunity to fundamentally improve how users discover products on HelloFresh:

- Enable intent-driven discovery for users who know what they want
- Reduce friction in the browsing experience
- Build a foundation for smarter, more personalized product discovery

I also created reusable components that scaled across the search experience.

## Competitive Benchmarking

I analyzed search experiences across meal kit competitors and adjacent food apps to understand patterns and opportunities:

**Meal Kit Competitors:**
- Blue Apron: Basic keyword search, limited filtering
- Dinnerly: No search functionality
- Marley Spoon: Simple search with category results
- EveryPlate: Browse-only experience

**Key Patterns:**
- Most competitors had basic or no search
- Grocery apps (Instacart, Amazon Fresh) set higher expectations
- Users expected search to understand intent, not just keywords

**Opportunity Gap:**
- Search could be a differentiator in the meal kit space
- Relevance was the key—not just matching keywords
- Integration with dietary preferences and past orders could add value

## Web v0 Learnings

Search had launched on web in the US and Canada the previous year. The results informed our mobile approach:

- Strong adoption: Users actively sought out search when available
- Relevance feedback: Users expected results to match their intent, not just contain keywords
- Query patterns: Most searches were for ingredients ("chicken", "salmon") or meal types ("quick", "vegetarian")

> "I searched for 'quick dinners' but got results with 'quick' in the title. I wanted meals I could make in under 30 minutes."
> — Web search user feedback

## Defining the Vision

Before diving into MVP features, I mapped out the long-term vision for menu discovery. Search was one piece of a larger puzzle: how do users find what they want across recipes, add-ons, and marketplace products?

## MVP Scoping

Working with Product and Engineering, we defined what would ship in the MVP versus what would come later:

**MVP scope:**
- Unified search across recipes and products
- Keyword matching with relevance ranking
- Search suggestions and recent searches
- Clear empty and error states

**Future iterations:**
- Semantic search (understanding intent beyond keywords)
- Personalized results based on preferences and history
- Voice search integration

## AI-Assisted Workflows

This project became a proving ground for integrating AI tools into my design workflow:

**UX Copy Iterations:** Used AI to rapidly generate and refine microcopy for search states—empty results, suggestions, error messages. This cut iteration time significantly while maintaining brand voice.

**Accessibility Audits:** AI-assisted accessibility reviews caught issues early—contrast ratios, screen reader flows, touch target sizes. Built accessibility into the design from the start rather than retrofitting.

**Illustration Generation:** Created placeholder illustrations for empty states and onboarding using AI tools, giving stakeholders a clear vision before investing in final assets.

**Prototype Development:** Used AI to accelerate interactive prototype creation, enabling faster user testing cycles and stakeholder alignment.

---

## The Solution

The final MVP delivered a search experience built on three principles:

**1. Accessible Entry Point:** A prominent search bar at the top of the Store, making it immediately discoverable. Users could tap to search from anywhere in the browsing experience.

**2. Smart Suggestions:** Recent searches and popular queries helped users start their search journey. Suggestions updated as users typed, showing relevant results before they finished typing.

**3. Relevant Results:** Results ranked by relevance, not just keyword matching. The algorithm considered product popularity, dietary tag matches, and seasonal relevance.

## Component Library Additions

The project added several reusable components to our design system:

- Search input with clear and voice input affordances
- Suggestion pills with recent/popular indicators
- Search result cards optimized for scanning
- Empty state illustrations and messaging

## Web Search Context

While mobile-specific metrics were still being collected at time of writing, the web v0 results provided strong validation for the mobile investment:

- Users who searched showed higher engagement with product pages
- Search users had higher add-to-cart rates than browse-only users
- Relevance improvements in later iterations correlated with conversion gains

## AI Workflow Impact

The AI-assisted workflows pioneered on this project have since been adopted across the design team:

- Faster iteration: UX copy cycles reduced from days to hours
- Earlier accessibility: Issues caught in design, not QA
- Better stakeholder alignment: Higher-fidelity prototypes earlier in the process

## What Worked

Vision-first approach: Mapping the long-term discovery vision before diving into MVP features helped maintain strategic alignment. Every MVP decision was evaluated against where we wanted to end up.

AI workflow integration: Treating this project as a testbed for AI tools paid off. The efficiency gains were real, and the learnings have scaled to other projects.

Web learnings: Having web search data meant we weren't starting from zero. We knew what queries to expect, what relevance issues to solve for, and what success looked like.

## What I'd Do Differently

- Earlier user research: We relied heavily on web data. Direct user research on mobile-specific search behaviors (voice input, one-handed use) could have informed the MVP better.
- Semantic search from the start: The technical complexity pushed semantic search to future iterations, but users expected it. A more aggressive push for intent-based search in MVP would have delivered more value.
- Metrics instrumentation: Better event tracking from day one would have given us faster feedback on what was working.
