City Transit Experience

End-to-end service design for a metropolitan transit system, improving accessibility and rider satisfaction across all touchpoints.

Role Service Designer
Timeline 8 months
Team Cross-functional team of 12
Methods Ethnographic Research, Systems Mapping, Accessibility Audit

Overview

The Challenge

A major metropolitan transit authority was facing declining ridership and poor accessibility ratings. The system had grown organically over decades, resulting in inconsistent experiences across different lines, stations, and services. They needed a unified service vision.

My Contribution

I was one of two service designers on this project. I led the accessibility workstream, conducted ethnographic research with diverse rider groups, and contributed to the overall service blueprint and design recommendations.

Research & Discovery

We conducted extensive field research over 6 weeks:

  • Ride-alongs: 40+ hours riding with diverse users including wheelchair users, blind riders, elderly passengers, and tourists
  • Station observations: Documented behavior at 15 stations during peak and off-peak hours
  • Staff interviews: Spoke with 20 frontline employees about daily challenges
  • Accessibility audit: Systematic evaluation of 8 major stations against WCAG and ADA guidelines

Field research: observing wayfinding behavior at a major interchange station

Key Insights

The most impactful finding was that accessibility issues affected far more than just disabled riders. Parents with strollers, travelers with luggage, and elderly riders all faced the same barriers. Designing for accessibility would improve the experience for everyone.

Process

Systems Mapping

Before jumping to solutions, we mapped the entire transit ecosystem—physical infrastructure, digital touchpoints, staff interactions, third-party services, and policy constraints. This revealed interdependencies that would affect any design intervention.

Systems map revealing connections between physical, digital, and human elements

Prioritization Framework

Given the scale of the system, we couldn't fix everything at once. I developed a prioritization framework based on impact (how many riders affected), effort (implementation complexity), and equity (whether it addressed accessibility gaps).

Solution

Our recommendations spanned short-term quick wins and longer-term systemic changes:

Unified Wayfinding System

A consistent signage and information hierarchy across all stations. This included tactile guidance paths, high-contrast signage, and multi-language support.

Real-Time Accessibility Information

Integration of elevator/escalator status into the trip planning app and station displays. Riders can now plan routes knowing which accessibility features are operational.

Staff Empowerment Program

Training and tools for frontline staff to assist riders with accessibility needs. This included disability awareness training and a quick-reference guide for common scenarios.

Inclusive Design Standards

A set of design standards for all future station renovations and new construction, ensuring accessibility is built in from the start.

Pilot implementation: new wayfinding system with tactile paths and high-contrast signage

Outcome

The phased implementation began with 5 pilot stations. After 6 months:

  • 28% increase in accessibility satisfaction scores at pilot stations
  • 15% reduction in staff-assisted navigation requests (riders could self-serve)
  • Adoption by city: Design standards incorporated into municipal transit policy
  • Recognition: Project shortlisted for Service Design Award

Reflection

Working on public infrastructure taught me patience and the value of incremental change. Unlike a startup where you can ship and iterate quickly, transit changes require years of planning, budget cycles, and stakeholder alignment.

The accessibility focus reinforced my belief in inclusive design. Every "accessibility feature" we implemented—clearer signage, real-time information, simpler navigation—benefited all riders. There's no tradeoff between accessible and good design.

I'm proud that the design standards we created will influence transit development for decades. It's a different kind of impact than shipping an app, but equally meaningful.
