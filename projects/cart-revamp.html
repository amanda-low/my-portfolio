Cart Revamp

Redesigning HelloFresh's cart for transparency, flexibility, and scale—shipped globally with +11.6% engagement with new delivery controls.

Role Product Designer (UX & Design)
Timeline 3-4 months
Scope iOS, Android, Web | HelloFresh + white-label brands
Methods User Research, Prototyping, A/B Testing, Cross-platform Design

Overview

The Challenge

The HelloFresh cart had become a friction point. Through pricing research and user interviews, we uncovered three recurring pain points:

  • "I want to know why certain add-ons are in my cart." — Users were confused by items appearing without context
  • "Show me how much I'm saving—it makes me feel good about my order." — Subscription benefits weren't visible
  • "I want more flexibility with my delivery without multiple steps." — Changing delivery required leaving the cart

Beyond user frustrations, the cart was a critical business touchpoint—where customers make pause and cancel decisions. With Factor integration on the roadmap, we needed a scalable foundation.

The Price Perception Problem

Our pricing pain point interviews revealed deeper issues around trust and transparency. Users felt uncertain about what they were paying for. Surprise charges led to distrust. Missing context about surcharges created friction.

"I just want to know what I'm getting and what I'm paying for. When things show up that I don't remember adding, it makes me question everything."

— User interview participant

My Contribution

I contributed to the research plan and partnered with our UX Researcher to conduct pricing pain point interviews. I owned the design variations, led stakeholder alignment on what to test vs. ship as baseline, and coordinated across iOS, Android, and Web platforms.

Research & Discovery

I synthesized insights from multiple research streams to build a complete picture:

Price Clarity Research

  • Users struggle to understand their total cost and what's included
  • Transparency about pricing builds trust and reduces anxiety
  • Savings visibility reinforces value perception and justifies the subscription

User Interview Themes

  • Frustration with unexpected charges and surcharges
  • Strong desire to see itemized breakdown of costs
  • Positive emotional response when seeing discounts and savings
  • Confusion about what add-ons are in cart and why

Usability Testing Insights

  • Users want clear explanation of additional fees
  • Context for why items appear matters significantly
  • Labels like "Paired with [meal name]" dramatically reduced confusion

User journey map highlighting pain points around pricing clarity

Key Insight: The cart wasn't just a checkout step—it was a moment of truth where users either felt confident in their decision or started questioning their subscription value.

Design Process

Defining the Scope

Working with Product and Engineering, we identified what should ship to everyone versus what needed validation:

Core enhancements (baseline for all):

  • Clickable food items with links to product details
  • Contextual add-on labels explaining why items are in cart
  • Improved cart structure and information hierarchy

Testable features:

  • Variation 1: Savings display showing subscription benefits
  • Variation 2: In-cart delivery date editing

Key Design Decisions

Why contextual add-on labels?

Research showed users were confused when items appeared unexpectedly. By adding labels like "Paired with Chicken Parmigiana," "Free Breakfast for Life," or "Subscribed to 1 per week," we provided immediate context that reduced friction and support inquiries.

Before/after: Contextual labels explain why items are in the cart

Why show savings from benefits only?

We scoped savings to subscription benefits rather than item-level discounts. This was partly a technical constraint, but also strategic—benefit savings reinforced subscription value, aligning with retention goals. Full price breakdown was planned for the 2026 pricing roadmap.

Why in-cart delivery edit without skip/pause?

Users wanted delivery flexibility, but adding skip/pause introduced complexity. We scoped to date changes only—solving the core need without the baggage of skip/cancel decisions that warranted separate consideration.

In-cart delivery editing: change dates without leaving the cart flow

Navigating Constraints

Technical Constraints

Savings display was limited to subscription benefits because item-level discount data wasn't available from the backend. Rather than delay, we scoped to what was achievable and documented the fuller vision for future work.

Cross-Team Dependencies

This project required coordination across Cart Experience Squad, Cart Foundation Squad, Product Analytics, and platform teams for iOS, Android, and Web implementation.

Stakeholder Alignment

A key challenge was aligning on what to test vs. ship as default. Through collaborative sessions, we agreed core enhancements would ship to everyone while savings and delivery features would be validated through experimentation.

The Solution

The final design introduced four key improvements:

1. Clickable Food Items

Every item links to its product detail page, letting users quickly verify what they're getting without leaving the cart.

2. Contextual Add-On Labels

Add-ons display clear labels: "Paired with Chicken Parmigiana," "Free Breakfast for Life," "Subscribed to 1 per week."

3. Savings Display

A collapsible section showing "$X saved!" with a link to the wallet drawer. Users see their subscription value at the moment of purchase decision.

4. In-Cart Delivery Reschedule

An "Edit" button opens a drawer to change delivery date—quick, contextual, and non-disruptive.

The redesigned cart with all four improvements in context

Results

We ran a controlled experiment with two variations to isolate feature impact.

Quantitative Results

Financial metrics were directionally positive but not statistically significant:

  • Net revenue: +0.23%
  • Net order value: +0.26%
  • Delivered orders: +0.08%
  • Pause rate: -0.39%

Behavioral Signals

The stronger story emerged from user engagement:

  • 11.6% of users clicked the Edit Delivery Drawer
  • +3.6% increase in users expanding the savings accordion
  • More delivery modifications observed across all markets
  • No negative impact on any metric in any market

Decision to Ship

While financial metrics weren't statistically significant, we rolled out globally based on:

  • Behavioral evidence: 11.6% delivery drawer engagement is substantial for a new UI element
  • No downside risk: Zero negative impact across any market or metric
  • Customer value delivered: We directly addressed documented pain points
  • Foundation for future: New architecture supports Factor integration
  • Research validation: Savings visibility patterns aligned with prior Loyalty rollout insights

Impact

Shipped to: iOS, Android, Web across all global markets (HelloFresh + white-label brands)

  • +11.6% engagement with delivery controls
  • +3.6% engagement with savings visibility
  • Increased delivery modifications across markets
  • Zero negative impact globally

Reflection

What Worked

Scoping carefully paid off. By separating "what we're confident about" from "what needs validation," we shipped user value quickly while generating learnings. The decision to roll out despite non-significant financial results was right—the absence of negative signal, combined with positive behavioral data and research confidence, was enough to move forward.

What I'd Do Differently

  • Longer experiment duration: Financial metrics like retention take time. A longer window might have captured downstream effects.
  • Item-level savings: The technical constraint left value on the table. I'd push harder for a phased approach to full price transparency.
  • Deeper delivery analysis: 11.6% engagement is promising, but understanding conversion—did users actually change dates, and did that reduce missed deliveries?—would strengthen the case.
